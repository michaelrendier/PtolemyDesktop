Definition

A Lie group G is simultaneously a smooth manifold and a group, with smooth multiplication and inversion:

μ: G×G → G, (g,h) ↦ gh (smooth)ι: G → G, g ↦ g⁻¹ (smooth)// Lie's theorem: every Lie group has a Lie algebra 𝔤 = T_e Gdim G = dim 𝔤 (as manifolds)
Geometric Intuition

Lie groups are the mathematical home of continuous symmetries. Every fundamental symmetry in physics — rotations, boosts, gauge transformations — is a Lie group. The group structure encodes how symmetries compose; the manifold structure means symmetries can be deformed continuously.

Relational Analysis — Density · Pull · Attraction

Symmetry density: the Haar measure on a Lie group is the unique (up to scale) translation-invariant measure. It's the "uniform density" on the group. Compact groups have finite Haar measure (normalize to 1). Non-compact groups (like boosts) have infinite measure — there's no uniform distribution over all boost velocities.

Three Levels
NOVICEHigh School · Continuous Symmetry is a Manifold
▶

Discrete symmetries (like flipping a sign) form finite groups. Continuous symmetries (like rotating by any angle) form Lie groups. SO(3) is the 3D sphere-surface of rotations — a 3D manifold. Every point on it is a rotation. Moving along it smoothly rotates your object smoothly. The Lie algebra is the tangent space at "no rotation".

JOURNEYMANCollegiate · Compact vs Non-Compact
▶

Compact Lie groups (SO(n), SU(n), Sp(n)): finite volume, all representations are unitary and finite-dimensional. Non-compact (SL(2,ℝ), Lorentz group): infinite volume, unitary representations are infinite-dimensional. Compact = bounded symmetry. Non-compact = unbounded (like boosts to arbitrary velocity).

MASTERGraduate · Peter-Weyl Theorem
▶

For a compact Lie group G, L²(G) decomposes as a direct sum over irreducible representations π: L²(G) = ⊕_π (dim π) · V_π. This generalizes Fourier series (L²(S¹) = ⊕ℂe^(inθ)) to all compact groups. The matrix coefficients of π form an orthogonal basis. This is why representation theory = harmonic analysis on groups.

Common Mistakes
MISTAKE 01
Confusing the Lie group with its Lie algebra. The group is curved (a manifold); the algebra is flat (a vector space).
MISTAKE 02
Assuming the exponential map is surjective. For SL(2,ℝ) it's not — not every matrix with det=1 is e^X.
MISTAKE 03
Thinking all Lie groups are matrix groups. Most are, but there are exceptional covering spaces that aren't.
