Dirac Equation

Definition

The Dirac equation for a spin-½ field ψ:

(iγμ∂μ − m)ψ = 0i∂̸ψ = mψ (∂̸ = Dirac operator)// ψ is a 4-component Dirac spinorSquaring: (□+m²)ψ = 0 (each component satisfies Klein-Gordon)// Predicts antimatter: negative energy solutions = positrons
Geometric Intuition

Dirac sought a relativistic, first-order wave equation. The Dirac operator squares to the Klein-Gordon operator. The 4-component structure is forced: 2 spin states × 2 particle/antiparticle states = 4. The equation predicted the positron (1928) before it was discovered (1932).

Relational Analysis — Density · Pull · Attraction

Spinor density: jμ = ψ̄γμψ is the conserved current with ρ = ψ†ψ > 0 always (unlike Klein-Gordon, where ρ can be negative). The probability density is positive-definite — the Dirac equation fixes the negative probability problem of Klein-Gordon for fermions.

Three Levels
NOVICEHigh School · The Equation That Predicted Antimatter
▶

Dirac was trying to write a relativistic wave equation that was first-order in time. He needed matrices that square to the identity and anticommute — gamma matrices. The equation had two solutions: positive energy (electron) and negative energy. Dirac interpreted negative energy solutions as a "sea" of filled states — holes in the sea are positrons. Antimatter was a mathematical necessity.

JOURNEYMANCollegiate · Chiral Symmetry
▶

In the massless limit m=0, the Dirac equation splits: (iγμ∂μ)ψ=0 decomposes into left-handed (ψ_L) and right-handed (ψ_R) Weyl spinors. Mass term mψ̄ψ = m(ψ̄_Rψ_L + ψ̄_Lψ_R) mixes chiralities. Massless Dirac = chiral symmetry. Mass breaks chiral symmetry — this is why the Higgs mechanism matters: it gives fermions mass without breaking gauge symmetry directly.

MASTERGraduate · QED Vertex
▶

In QED, the interaction is L_int = −eψ̄γμψAμ. The vertex factor is −ieγμ. The full propagator is S(p) = i(p̸+m)/(p²−m²+iε). The Dirac equation in momentum space: (p̸−m)u(p) = 0 for positive-energy spinors. u(p) has two solutions (spin up, spin down). v(p) has two antiparticle solutions.

Common Mistakes
MISTAKE 01
Confusing Dirac equation (first-order, 4-component) with Klein-Gordon (second-order, 1-component). Use Dirac for fermions, Klein-Gordon for bosons.
MISTAKE 02
Forgetting that ψ is a 4-component spinor, not a scalar. Each component satisfies Klein-Gordon but they're mixed by the gamma matrices.
MISTAKE 03
Interpreting negative-energy solutions as problems. They're physical — they're antiparticles.
