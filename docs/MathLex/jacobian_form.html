Definition

The Jacobian of F: ℝn → ℝm at x is the m×n matrix:

JF(x) = [∂Fi/∂xj] (i=row, j=col)det(JF) = local volume scaling factor// F(x+δ) ≈ F(x) + J_F(x)·δ (linear approximation)
Geometric Intuition — What It Does

The Jacobian is the best linear approximation to F at x. det(J) tells you how much F stretches or compresses volumes locally. det(J)>0: orientation-preserving. det(J)<0: orientation-reversing. det(J)=0: the map collapses a dimension — critical point.

Relational Analysis — Density · Pull · Attraction

Local density: det(J) is the ratio of output volume to input volume. In change of variables ∫f(F(x))|det J|dx = ∫f(y)dy. The Jacobian converts density in one coordinate system to density in another. This is how you transform probability distributions under nonlinear maps.

Three Levels of Understanding
NOVICE High School · The Best Local Approximation
▶

Zoom in on any smooth map at a point. It looks linear. The Jacobian is that linear map. If you zoom in on a globe at a city, the map projection looks flat and undistorted — that's the Jacobian. The determinant tells you how much the area is being stretched right there.

JOURNEYMAN Collegiate · Inverse Function Theorem
▶

If det(J_F(x₀)) ≠ 0, then F is locally invertible near x₀. The inverse has Jacobian J_F⁻¹ = (J_F)⁻¹. This is the fundamental theorem for local coordinate changes. Critical points (det J = 0) are where the map folds — no local inverse exists.

MASTER Graduate / PhD · Pushforward and Pullback
▶

The Jacobian is the matrix of the pushforward map F*: T_xM → T_{F(x)}N between tangent spaces. Its transpose is the pullback F*: T*_{F(x)}N → T*_xM. In differential geometry: F*(g) = J^T g J (pullback of metric). The Cauchy-Riemann equations say J is a conformal matrix (scalar × rotation).

Derivation
// Polar coordinate JacobianF(r,θ) = (r cosθ, r sinθ) J = [[cosθ, −r sinθ],[sinθ, r cosθ]] det(J) = r (area element dA = r dr dθ)
Hello World — Simplest Instantiation
F(x,y) = (x²−y², 2xy) (squaring in ℂ)J = [[2x,−2y],[2y,2x]], det = 4(x²+y²) = 4|z|²

Common Mistakes
MISTAKE 01
Confusing the Jacobian (rectangular matrix) with the Jacobian determinant (scalar). They're related but different.
MISTAKE 02
Using the Jacobian beyond the range where F is approximately linear. The approximation breaks down at large δ.
MISTAKE 03
Forgetting that for complex functions, the Jacobian being conformal (scalar × rotation) is equivalent to the Cauchy-Riemann equations.
