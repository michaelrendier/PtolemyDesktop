## Biography

Michael Faraday was born to a poor blacksmith in London. He received almost no formal education. At 14 he was apprenticed to a bookbinder and educated himself by reading the books he bound. He attended Humphry Davy's lectures at the Royal Institution, took notes, bound them, and sent them to Davy as a demonstration of seriousness. Davy hired him as a laboratory assistant.

He went on to become the greatest experimental physicist of the 19th century. He discovered electromagnetic induction, the laws of electrolysis, diamagnetism, the magneto-optical effect (Faraday effect), and introduced the concept of the electromagnetic field. He invented the electric motor, the dynamo, the transformer, and the Faraday cage.

He had no formal mathematics. He could not write the equations that described what he discovered. Maxwell translated Faraday's physical intuition into mathematics — the field concept became Maxwell's equations. Faraday had it right; Maxwell formalized it.

## Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction (1831)

A changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an electromotive force (EMF) in that loop:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

where $\Phi_B = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$ is the magnetic flux through a surface $S$ bounded by the loop.

In differential (Maxwell) form:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

The negative sign is Lenz's law: the induced EMF opposes the change in flux that caused it. The field resists being changed. This is not friction — it is geometry.

## Maxwell's Formalization

James Clerk Maxwell (1865) translated Faraday's physical field concept into differential equations. The four Maxwell equations include Faraday's law as one of their number. Maxwell himself wrote:

> "The method which Faraday employed in his researches... I have found to be capable of being expressed in mathematical form."

The electromagnetic field is not a property of space — it is a geometric object that can change, and its rate of change produces force. This is the conceptual leap that Faraday made without mathematics and Maxwell formalized with it.
